SendMoi for iOS & macOS

Reimagining a Beloved Utility, With Help From Codex & Claude Code

A focused send-to-self app for Apple platforms, built to replace a discontinued utility I loved and to test whether AI coding tools could help carry a strong product vision into polished native software.

Role

Indie Product Designer / Engineer

Organization

Self

Timeframe

February 2026 – Present

Platforms

iPhone, iPad, macOS Share Extension

A Workflow I Missed Enough To Rebuild

SendMoi exists for a simple reason: I missed Mailo. It was a small mail-to-self utility I used constantly because it made an existing habit feel fast, dependable, and almost invisible. When something actually mattered, emailing it to yourself was still one of the simplest ways to make sure it stayed yours, and Mailo understood that workflow better than most tools did.

Then it disappeared. As the archived farewell note explain, Alex Mabe's Ameba shut Mailo down after Google policy changes made its Gmail-native model impractical for a small team. The product went away, but the behavior did not. I still wanted a dependable send-to-self path from Apple's share sheet, one that turned links, text, and images into readable emails instead of broken fragments and still felt trustworthy when connectivity was weak. Too often the default path meant a titleless URL, a clipped image, or a blob of text dropped into the inbox with no care at all.

That made the opportunity more interesting to me than a larger productivity concept. The goal was not to invent a new category or build another bloated capture app. It was to rebuild a narrow, high-frequency utility I already knew I wanted, then make it feel more intentional, trustworthy, and production-grade.

The project also changed the way I thought about execution. I am not a traditional iOS or macOS developer, but Codex and Claude Code made it possible to close the gap between product judgment and native implementation quickly enough to seriously explore the idea. SendMoi became both a product exercise and a test: could a beloved discontinued workflow, paired with AI-assisted development, become a polished native app instead of stopping at a prototype?

The Product Got Better As The Brief Got Smaller

The fastest way to make the product worse would have been to broaden the brief too early. There were plenty of adjacent directions available: folders, collaboration, annotations, tagging, a fuller archive, maybe even a lightweight reading interface. All of them could have sounded strategic on paper and diluted the actual value. The core promise stayed simple: when something moves through SendMoi, it should feel safer, cleaner, and more dependable than the default path.

That led to a few design principles early. The share extension had to be treated as the real front door, not a satellite surface. Delivery could not be best effort; the moment the user committed, the work had to become durable. And the output itself had to earn the extra step. If the final email looked no better than a raw forwarded URL, the product would feel ornamental instead of useful.

Those principles also created a practical way to work with Codex and Claude Code. The tools were most effective when the product intent stayed sharp. Instead of asking them to invent the app, I used them to accelerate implementation paths, test interface ideas, explore system behavior, and move through unfamiliar native details without lowering the product bar.

The Hard Part Began After Dismiss

One of the most important product decisions was invisible: treat every outbound message as queued work before attempting delivery. That sounds implementation-heavy, but it is really interaction design. The user should be able to send, dismiss the sheet, and trust that the product now owns the uncertainty.

Once framed that way, a lot of downstream decisions became clearer. Retry behavior, local persistence, shared state between the extension and main app, and status communication were all part of the same product problem. Reliability was not a backend detail hidden beneath the interface. It was the interface. This was also where AI-assisted building proved especially useful, making it possible to iterate on native behavior that otherwise would have been slower, less familiar, or easy to avoid.

The Best Version Disappeared Into The Action

The middle phase of the project was less about generating lots of visual directions and more about pressure-testing the edges of the workflow. How little information could the product ask for on repeat use? How aggressively could it infer a default recipient? Where did automation start to feel helpful instead of presumptuous? The optional auto-send path came out of that thinking. For a user repeatedly sending to the same address, the best version of the product is often the one that disappears into the action: share, confirm, move on.

I also spent time refining what the email should become once the send was safe. A lot of shared content is structurally noisy: inconsistent titles, missing preview images, publisher-specific formatting, or content blobs that collapse badly in email clients. That made cleanup and presentation a meaningful design layer, not polish at the end. The final artifact needed to feel worth receiving, with enough structure that the inbox itself became a useful lightweight archive.

This is where the combination of product judgment and AI-assisted building became especially effective. Product design set the bar and the priorities; Codex and Claude Code helped turn those decisions into real iteration loops across the extension, state handling, content formatting, and app behavior. The resulting product is intentionally narrow, but not simplistic. Gmail OAuth reduces setup friction. A default-recipient model shortens repeat behavior. The share extension captures links, text, images, and app-provided metadata. The main app handles recovery and review.

That is also where the principal-level value lives in a project like this. The work is not interesting because it is large. It is interesting because the product judgment stays coherent across framing, interaction design, implementation constraints, content shaping, and system behavior, even while the build process itself is being learned in real time.

Core Principle

If a user can dismiss the share sheet in a second, the product has to do the worrying for them.

Reliability Was The Product, Not The Cleanup Phase

Because the product promise is reliability, QA could not be treated as a final sweep. The work had to be tested across the failure modes that define whether a utility earns trust: variable connectivity, inconsistent share payloads, extension lifecycle interruptions, empty or partial metadata, and the handoff between extension state and main-app recovery. The design work and the quality work were tightly entangled.

Handoff also looked different here because the same person shaping the product vision was using Codex and Claude Code to help carry it into implementation. Instead of a traditional design handoff package, the key artifact was a clear internal model of states, edge cases, and failure handling that could survive implementation detail. That made it possible to move between interface decisions and system decisions without losing the product thread.

The project reinforced something I believe more strongly every year: in focused products, users often experience architecture as tone. A queue that survives failure feels calm. A noisy setup flow feels needy. A polished final email feels respectful. The technology matters, but what the user perceives is whether the product behaves like it has its act together. That is exactly why this project mattered to me as an AI-assisted build. The standard was never “did the code compile?” It was “does this feel like a real product someone could trust with something they did not want to lose?”

What SendMoi Proved

SendMoi is a useful example of end-to-end product design at small scale. The scope is deliberately tight, but the work touches research, framing, ideation, systems thinking, interface design, implementation, QA, and refinement. More specifically, it shows what can happen when a clear product vision is paired with AI-assisted development in a disciplined way: not code for code's sake, but a faster path from intent to working software.

More concretely, the product delivers a dependable send-to-self workflow across Apple platforms without pretending to be a larger productivity suite. Users can share once, move on quickly, and trust that the app will either deliver immediately or preserve the work for retry. The output is cleaner, the setup is lighter, and the interaction feels more dependable than the habit it replaces.

The broader lesson is worth carrying forward: small utilities earn their place by being precise, not by acting bigger than they are. AI coding tools are at their best when they serve that precision, helping a strong product idea become a polished native app instead of a vague demo.
