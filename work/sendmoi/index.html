SendMoi

Designing a Durable Send-to-Self Utility

SendMoi is a focused utility for one recurring behavior: saving what matters by sending it to your own inbox.

Role

Indie Product Designer / Engineer

Organization

SendMoi

Timeframe

February 2026 – Present

Platforms

iPhone, iPad, macOS Share Extension

The Habit Already Existed

SendMoi started with a behavior I already trusted more than most read-later systems: if something mattered, I emailed it to myself. The behavior was simple, durable, and already woven into the way people manage their own attention. The problem was that the tooling around that habit felt improvised. Share sheets dropped content. Links arrived as ugly fragments. Weak connectivity made the action feel uncertain. The promise was personal, but the product experience around it was flimsy.

That tension made the opportunity more interesting to me than a larger productivity concept. I was not trying to invent a new category or replace a notes app. I wanted to make a narrow, high-frequency workflow feel deliberate and trustworthy enough that a user could act quickly, move on, and believe the system would do the rest.

Framed that way, the project became less about shipping a "send to self" feature and more about designing confidence. What should happen when the share extension gets thin metadata? What happens when the network drops after the user has already dismissed the sheet? How much setup can the product ask for before it stops feeling like a utility and starts feeling like admin? Those were product questions as much as technical ones.

Defining The Design Problem As Trust

The fastest way to make this product worse would have been to broaden the brief too early. There were plenty of adjacent directions available: folders, collaboration, annotations, tagging, a fuller archive, maybe even a lightweight reading interface. All of those ideas could have sounded strategic on paper and diluted the actual value. I kept coming back to one product promise: when you send something through SendMoi, it should feel safer and cleaner than the default path, not more ambitious.

That led to a few design principles early. First, the share extension had to be treated as the real front door, not a satellite surface. If the action begins in Safari, Photos, Notes, or any other app, the experience has to succeed there rather than forcing a clumsy handoff back into the main app. Second, delivery could not be best effort. A send needed to become durable work the moment the user committed, even if the network or extension lifecycle made immediate completion impossible. Third, the output itself had to earn the extra step. If the final email looked no better than a forwarded raw URL, the whole product would feel ornamental.

Those principles gave me a way to evaluate ideas during exploration. Every feature had to either reduce friction, increase trust, or improve the quality of the final artifact. If it did none of those, it was probably outside the brief.

Designing For The Moment After Dismiss

One of the most important product decisions was invisible: treat every outbound message as queued work before attempting delivery. That sounds implementation-heavy, but it is really interaction design. The user should be able to send, close the sheet, and trust that the product now owns the uncertainty.

Once I framed it that way, a lot of downstream decisions became clearer. Retry behavior, local persistence, shared state between the extension and main app, and status communication were all part of the same design problem. Reliability was not a backend detail hidden beneath the interface. It was the interface.

Exploration, Narrowing, And Refinement

The middle phase of the project was less about generating lots of visual directions and more about pressure-testing the edges of the workflow. I explored how little information the product could ask for on repeat use, how aggressively it could infer a default recipient, and where automation started to feel helpful instead of presumptuous. The optional auto-send path came out of that thinking. For a user who is repeatedly sending to the same address, the best version of the product is often the one that lets them finish in a heartbeat.

I also spent time refining what the email should become once the send was safe. A lot of shared content is structurally noisy: inconsistent titles, missing preview images, publisher-specific formatting, or content blobs that collapse badly in email clients. That made cleanup and presentation a meaningful design layer, not polish at the end. I wanted the final artifact to feel like something worth receiving, with enough structure that the inbox itself became a useful lightweight archive.

The resulting product is intentionally narrow, but it is not simplistic. Gmail OAuth reduces setup friction. A default-recipient model shortens repeat behavior. The share extension captures links, text, images, and app-provided metadata. The main app handles recovery and review. Each decision was in service of the same idea: keep the promise small, then make that promise unusually well-resolved.

That is also where the principal-level value lives in a project like this. The work is not impressive because it is large. It is impressive, if it succeeds, because the product judgment stays coherent across research, interaction design, implementation constraints, content shaping, and system behavior.

Core Principle

If a user can dismiss the share sheet in a second, the product has to do the worrying for them.

QA, Handoff, And The Unsexy Parts Of Utility Design

Because the product promise is reliability, QA could not be treated as a final sweep. I needed to test across the failure modes that define whether a utility earns trust: variable connectivity, inconsistent share payloads, extension lifecycle interruptions, empty or partial metadata, and the handoff between extension state and main-app recovery. The "design" work and the "quality" work were deeply entangled.

Handoff looked different here because I was also building the product. Instead of a traditional design handoff package, the important artifact was a clear internal model of states, edge cases, and failure handling that could survive implementation detail. That let me move between interface decisions and system decisions without losing the product thread.

The project reinforced something I believe more strongly every year: in focused products, users often experience architecture as tone. A queue that survives failure feels calm. A noisy setup flow feels needy. A polished final email feels respectful. The technology matters, but what the user perceives is whether the product behaves like it has its act together.

What This Project Proved

SendMoi now reads to me as a useful example of end-to-end product design at small scale. The scope is deliberately tight, but the work touches research, framing, ideation, systems thinking, interface design, implementation, QA, and refinement. It demonstrates the kind of judgment I care most about: knowing when to narrow, where to invest, and how to make technical decisions show up as a better user experience instead of extra complexity.

More concretely, the product delivers a durable send-to-self workflow across Apple platforms without pretending to be a larger productivity suite. Users can share once, move on quickly, and trust that the app will either deliver immediately or preserve the work for retry. The output is cleaner, the setup is lighter, and the interaction feels more dependable than the default habit it replaces.

The broader learning is one I keep returning to: small utilities earn their place by being precise, not by acting bigger than they are. When the brief is narrow and the execution respects every edge case, even a tiny workflow can feel like infrastructure.
