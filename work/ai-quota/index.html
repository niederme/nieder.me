AIQuota for macOS

The Limits Were Real, The Signals Were Not: A Heads-Up Visualization of AI Tool Usage

AI-assisted coding became part of my daily workflow quickly, but the feedback loop around usage stayed oddly primitive.

Role

Indie Product Designer / Engineer

Organization

AIQuota

Timeframe

March 2026 – Present

Status

Shipped, open source, actively iterating

Platforms

macOS menu bar app, widgets, settings utility

A New Workflow Pain Was Showing Up Faster Than The Tooling

AIQuota came out of a very current kind of interruption. Once Codex and Claude Code became part of my normal practice, quota state started shaping decisions in the middle of active work. Could I keep pushing on this thread? Should I switch tools? Was I about to hit a limit without realizing it? What had looked like an account detail started behaving like workflow infrastructure.

The available answer was surprisingly clumsy. To understand my state, I usually had to leave the editor, open a browser, navigate to account pages, and translate two very different usage models by hand. That friction felt out of proportion to the question. The need was lightweight and frequent. The existing solution was heavyweight and interruptive.

That is what made the product direction clear so quickly. This did not need to be a dashboard. It needed to be a native utility that lived where the question actually appears: in the background of active work. Put the answer in the menu bar, make it legible at a glance, and keep the setup path calm enough that the product feels like part of macOS instead of a sidecar admin tool.

Research Started With Translation, Not Fetching

The hard part was never just pulling usage data. The deeper design challenge was that OpenAI and Anthropic describe limits in fundamentally different ways, and those differences shape the user's mental model. Codex leans on a weekly cap with approximate message counts. Claude combines a 5-hour rate window, a 7-day allowance, and Max-plan overflow credits. A literal presentation of both would be accurate and cognitively noisy.

So the first design task was comparative research and normalization. What is the common question across both systems? Which details are essential for quick decision-making, and which only matter after the user has opened the popover? How much provider-specific nuance should stay visible so the product feels trustworthy rather than falsely simplified? I treated that translation layer as core product design, not as presentation polish after the data model existed.

That framing also shaped trust decisions elsewhere. Authentication through an existing browser session, secure token storage in Keychain, resilient refresh behavior, clear reset timing, and threshold-based notifications all affect whether a utility like this feels dependable or disposable. In products this small, trust is often built from details users would struggle to name individually.

Glanceability Came Before Dashboard Thinking

One of the key product choices was resisting the temptation to over-explain on the first surface. The menu bar icon should answer the ambient question before the popover even opens, and the popover should extend that answer rather than restart the story with a wall of detail.

That led to a layered model: ambient status first, compact comparison second, richer account context third. It is a simple hierarchy, but it is what keeps the app feeling like a native utility instead of a miniature analytics product.

Building A Visual Grammar For Unlike Systems

Once the translation problem was clear, the interface needed a grammar that could make unlike provider models feel coherent without implying they were identical. Dual-arc gauges, plan badges, credits, window labels, reset times, and freshness indicators became the vocabulary. The point was not to make everything look the same. It was to make the act of reading the information feel consistent.

Color and motion played a supporting role here. As usage approaches a limit, the gauge shifts from calm to warning states before the quota is actually exhausted. That sounds minor, but it changes the product from passive reporting into a more actionable system. Instead of learning only when the answer becomes "no," the user gets enough runway to decide whether to keep pushing, switch tools, or pause.

That led to a few explicit product decisions. The gauges emphasize percent used rather than raw remaining credits because the primary question is risk, not accounting. Warning thresholds are early on purpose, so the app creates runway before exhaustion rather than simply confirming it. The menu bar owns the first answer because it is the lowest-friction checkpoint during active work, and widgets extend the same language into more ambient contexts without turning the product into a dashboard.

I also kept refining the boundary between useful specificity and clutter. The best version of this product is not the one with the most fields. It is the one that makes the next decision obvious in a glance, then rewards a closer look with just enough supporting context to feel trustworthy.

Outside Signal

“It solves a clear problem, stays within its bounds, and doesn’t overreach.”

Refinement After Launch Was About Calm, Not Expansion

Once the first version was working, the next phase was not about piling on adjacent features. It was about making the utility feel increasingly well-behaved. Widgets extended the product into more ambient contexts. Notifications became more useful as thresholds and language tightened. Network recovery and refresh handling reduced the amount of babysitting the product required. Silent auto-update behavior helped the app feel more like infrastructure and less like a hobby project demanding attention.

A lot of that refinement work sat below the visual layer. Polling and cache freshness had to feel accurate without becoming noisy. Notifications needed enough lead time to be useful without turning into alert fatigue. Widgets, popover state, and settings all had to stay in sync across refreshes, failures, and recovery. In a utility this small, QA is product design.

That refinement loop is important to me because it reflects a specific product philosophy. Mature utility design is often about subtraction, sequencing, and restraint. You can feel whether a product is trying to be admired or trying to be relied on. AIQuota gets stronger each time it becomes easier to ignore until the moment it is needed.

The open-source release helped validate that direction as well. Shipping publicly created a tighter feedback loop around setup clarity, edge cases, and what kinds of details people actually cared about once they started integrating the tool into their own workflow.

What Shipping AIQuota Taught Me

AIQuota shipped as a notarized macOS app with menu bar monitoring, widgets, notifications, and support for both Codex and Claude Code. The current release is available as an open-source GitHub project with downloadable desktop builds, and it continues to evolve through polish passes that improve clarity more than breadth.

More importantly, it validated a product pattern I care about. As AI tools become everyday infrastructure, a whole layer of adjacent workflow problems appears around them. Some of the best opportunities are not bigger assistants, but thinner native utilities that remove friction at the edges of real working habits.

This project also sharpened my belief that small utilities still demand strong product judgment. The right answer was not maximum detail, feature sprawl, or a billing-style dashboard. It was a calm, legible answer to a very human question in the middle of active work: am I safe to keep going?
